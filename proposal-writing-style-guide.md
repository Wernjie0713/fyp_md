# Proposal Writing Style Guide

This guide captures the writing style and formatting conventions used in the current FYP draft. All future revisions and newly generated content should follow these rules so the document reads as if it were written by a single author.

## Purpose

Use this guide when revising any section of the proposal draft, especially when incorporating supervisor feedback. The goal is stylistic consistency, not stylistic improvement for its own sake.

## 1. Tone and Voice

- Use formal academic English.
- Maintain a neutral, impersonal, and professional tone.
- Present ideas in a measured and evidence-led manner.
- Avoid conversational, promotional, emotional, or exaggerated phrasing.
- Avoid sounding overly definitive when a point is still under development or validation.
- Do not use first-person narration such as `I`, `we`, `our`, or `my` inside the draft.

Preferred subject patterns:

- `the project`
- `the proposed platform`
- `the system`
- `this chapter`
- `the current stage`
- `the current project phase`

## 2. Certainty and Claim Framing

The current draft uses cautious and defensible wording. Preserve that pattern.

Preferred phrasing:

- `the project proposes`
- `the platform is designed to`
- `the proposed approach adopts`
- `the current stage focuses on`
- `the project targets`
- `the design prioritises`
- `planned for subsequent iterations`
- `implemented and validated`
- `deployed to a cloud-hosted development environment`

Avoid:

- absolute claims without support
- sales-like language such as `powerful`, `innovative`, `cutting-edge`, or `revolutionary`
- overclaiming completion when the draft clearly presents work as ongoing

## 3. Standard Terminology

Use the exact core terms already established in the draft unless a supervisor explicitly asks for different wording.

- `Sales and Payment Analytics Platform`
- `vendor-managed POS reporting portal`
- `company-owned reporting redundancy system`
- `1:1 replication`
- `Microsoft SQL Server`
- `ELT workflow`
- `semantic/API layer`
- `schema-on-read`
- `parity validation`
- `reconciliation`
- `continuity reporting`
- `read-only reporting environment`
- `Finance and Operations users`

Do not casually replace these with alternate labels if the meaning is the same.

## 4. Spelling and Language Conventions

Follow the British/Commonwealth spelling already used in the draft.

Examples:

- `organisation`
- `prioritise`
- `behaviour`
- `modelling`
- `authorisation`

Use standard technical product names exactly as written by their vendors, for example `FastAPI`, `React`, and `SQL Server`.

## 5. Paragraph Style

- Keep paragraphs compact but information-dense.
- Usually begin with context, then explain the rationale, then state the implication or limitation.
- Use smooth transitions between ideas instead of abrupt topic changes.
- Avoid filler sentences that do not add analytical value.
- Avoid very short fragment paragraphs unless used intentionally for structure.

Typical paragraph pattern:

1. Introduce the topic or context.
2. Explain why it matters in this project.
3. Qualify the claim with scope, limitation, or current status.

## 6. Section Openings and Chapter Flow

Most sections begin with a short orienting paragraph before moving into detailed subsections, tables, or figures. Preserve that structure.

Preferred opening patterns:

- `This chapter presents...`
- `This chapter reviews...`
- `This chapter describes...`
- `The proposed system is organised into...`
- `The project adopts...`

When revising one section, maintain logical continuity with the surrounding chapter.

## 7. Status Reporting Rules

The draft carefully distinguishes between completed work, current implementation, and future work. Keep those distinctions precise.

Use these labels consistently:

- `implemented`
- `validated`
- `deployed`
- `planned`
- `under subsequent iterations`
- `as of Week 28 of the internship`

Do not collapse these into vague wording such as `done` or `already completed` unless the surrounding text uses that exact level of certainty.

## 8. Scope and Limitation Language

The draft regularly defines boundaries to avoid overclaiming.

Preferred patterns:

- `The platform is designed as a read-only reporting environment...`
- `The current project phase targets...`
- `Inventory and customer relationship management (CRM) analytics are excluded from the current scope...`
- `Final sizing depends on...`
- `The intent of this evaluation is to confirm operational acceptability rather than to conduct exhaustive benchmarking...`

When adding content, explicitly state exclusions, assumptions, or constraints where relevant.

## 9. Academic Framing and Citations

In literature and methodology sections:

- support conceptual claims with citations
- synthesise sources rather than stacking unsupported assertions
- connect literature to project choices explicitly

In design and analysis sections:

- focus more on design rationale and internal consistency
- use chapter and section cross-references where helpful
- avoid introducing literature citations unless the surrounding section already relies on them

## 10. Stakeholder and Organisational Language

- Use role-based descriptions rather than informal personal descriptions.
- Refer to `Finance`, `Operations`, and `internal technical team` consistently.
- Avoid speculative organisational claims, especially about hierarchy or policy, unless already established in the draft.

## 11. Tables, Figures, and Structured Content

The current draft uses tables heavily for structured information. Continue that pattern where appropriate.

Use tables for:

- requirements
- progress summaries
- comparative discussions
- schedules
- report specifications
- component responsibilities

Figure handling:

- place the figure reference on its own line
- follow it with an italicised caption
- keep caption wording formal and descriptive

## 12. Sentence Style

- Prefer full grammatical sentences with moderate complexity.
- Use technical precision without becoming overly dense.
- Use list formatting only when the surrounding chapter already benefits from it.
- Avoid rhetorical questions and dramatic phrasing.
- Avoid sudden shifts between highly technical language and casual explanation.

## 13. Revision Rules for New Content

When generating new content to insert into the draft:

- match the terminology used in adjacent paragraphs
- match the certainty level used in adjacent paragraphs
- match the section's citation density
- preserve any existing chronology, especially references to Week 28 status
- avoid introducing new concepts unless they are clearly connected to existing scope

## 14. Quick Do/Do Not Guide

Do:

- write formally and consistently
- qualify claims carefully
- anchor content to project scope
- maintain traceability between problem, method, design, and validation
- preserve the existing technical narrative

Do not:

- make the writing sound like a different author
- over-polish into a noticeably different voice
- add generic AI-style filler
- replace established technical terms with unnecessary synonyms
- introduce unsupported certainty

## 15. Reusable Sentence Patterns

These patterns reflect the current draft and may be reused when appropriate:

- `This chapter presents the analysis and design of the proposed...`
- `The project adopts an iterative and incremental development approach...`
- `The platform is scoped as a read-only reporting environment...`
- `The proposed approach combines...`
- `The design prioritises traceability, repeatability, and operational controls...`
- `Validation is performed through parity checks against vendor exports...`
- `As of Week 28 of the internship...`
- `Automation is planned for subsequent iterations.`

## 16. Revision Summary Marker

To help confirm that this guide is being followed during collaboration:

- In assistant summaries of completed changes shared in chat, address the user as `dear`.
- This rule applies to revision summaries and change updates only.
- This rule does not apply to the proposal draft itself and must not be inserted into academic content unless explicitly requested.

## Final Rule

If a revision reads more polished but no longer sounds like the surrounding chapter, it is not acceptable. Consistency with the current draft takes priority over stylistic reinvention.
